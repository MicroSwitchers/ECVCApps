The Workshop

Welcome to my digital workshop, a collection of my ongoing projects dedicated to exploring technology in blind and low-vision early intervention. This space serves as a workbench for ideas, featuring a mix of 3D-printed tools, vibe-coded web applications, and microcontroller-based devices.

Some of these projects are polished and may be ready for everyday use, while others are active experiments and proofs-of-concept. The goal is to innovate and share possibilities, so please feel free to explore, adapt, and build upon these projects.

Please see each project's GitHub page for licensing information. Most are released under permissive open-source licenses, such as MIT, to encourage building off these ideas.

Mechanical Braille Writer Simulator

Practice using a mechanical Braille writer with this interactive simulator. It emulates the writer's features and limitations to help build muscle memory, while conserving paper. The app supports two input methods: use the sdf and jkl keys on a standard keyboard, or press the virtual keys directly on a tablet's screen. For the best experience using touch on a tablet, install the app to your device from your web browser's settings and use full-screen landscape mode.

Use the Mechanical Braille Writer Simulator
  • Braille
  • Keyboard

SixKey - Braille Practice Pad

A minimal Braille writing jot pad for practicing 6 key input with a standard computer keyboard using the sdf and jkl keys to produce braille cells. The app is intended to be small and efficient with minimal features or distractions. A dark mode is provided to reduce eye strain, a zoom feature can enlarge the cells, and an import and export feature allows the text to be saved and opened. It exports a standard Unicode (.txt) file, allowing you to open your work as Braille cells in any basic text editor.

Use the SixKey - Braille Practice Pad
  • Braille
  • Tablet
  • Keyboard

BrailleFlex

Practice Braille with flexible key placement. This app works with a keyboard's `sdf` and `jkl` keys, or with adjustable on-screen touch keys that can be shifted for a more comfortable position on a tablet. It's a quick and basic tool for practicing 6 key input visually and not a full featured Braille app. For best results using the touch keys install the app through your browser and use landscape mode in full screen.

Use BrailleFlex

The Alligator Switch

The Alligator Switch is a 3D-printable access tool designed to support individuals with unique movement patterns. Its low-profile, contoured shape allows it to be positioned at various angles under a user‚Äôs hand to find the most ergonomic fit, while the activation mechanism requires minimal movement.

This print-in-place design is printed as a single unit. With the addition of a tactile switch and a cord, the entire assembly consists of only three parts. This project is ideal for makers with moderate 3D printing experience and basic soldering skills.

‚ñ∂ üõ†Ô∏è Build Specifications & Materials
  • Material: PETG ‚Äì Recommended for its durability. PLA is more prone to cracking under the repeated pressure applied to the hinges.
  • Print Orientation: Upright ‚Äì Position with hinges on the base so print lines orbit the hinges rather than passing through them for maximum strength.
  • Tactile Switch: Omron B3F-4055 (or similar) ‚Äì Chosen for its specific push-back force. Note: If using a different switch, you may need to adjust the base tolerances.
  • Cord: 3.5mm Male Mono Jack ‚Äì Or a 3.5mm mono plug cord/patch cable to allow for adjustable lengths.
  • Adhesive: Hot Glue ‚Äì Used to secure the switch and the cable in its channel.
‚ñ∂ üìñ Assembly & Pro-Tips

Switch Placement: Use tweezers to carefully pry the switch open and place it inside. A small bead of hot glue on the bottom of the switch can help raise it by a millimeter to achieve the perfect activation feel.

Testing & Adjustment: Always test the tactile switch activation before gluing. The design intentionally leaves some space for builders to dial in the perfect activation feel. If needed (and only if needed), a bead of hot glue or a small shim can boost the switch up by a millimeter or two.

Usage: When positioned correctly, the user can rest their hand gently on the switch, and a small movement will activate it.

Feedback: When calibrated correctly, the switch provides a highly satisfying tactile and auditory "click."

Safety Note

All single switches pose a potential choking hazard due to the cord or risk of breakage. This device should be used under supervision with children or any individual at risk of placing small parts in their mouth.
